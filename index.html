Projects & Explorations

Tower of Hanoi and Recursive Structure
I explored the Tower of Hanoi as an illustration of recursive reasoning. The optimal solution for n disks can be constructed by solving two smaller problems of size n−1, revealing a self-similar structure. Studying this process strengthened my understanding of recursion, mathematical induction, and algorithmic decomposition — how complex behaviour can emerge from simple recursive rules.
Lazy Susan Puzzle and Algorithmic Strategy
I studied the Lazy Susan coin-flipping puzzle, where the state of the table is hidden by arbitrary rotation. The challenge is to design a deterministic sequence of moves that guarantees a solution regardless of the unknown rotation. I implemented solver strategies in Python and investigated how patterns discovered in small cases can be extended systematically to tables of size 2ⁿ.
Algorithmic Thinking
These explorations reflect my interest in how mathematical reasoning can guide algorithm design. Rather than treating puzzles as isolated problems, I focused on identifying structural patterns — recursion, symmetry, and invariants — that make general solutions possible.
Programs as Mathematical Objects
I am interested in how programs can be understood through mathematical logic. Rather than viewing code only as instructions for a computer, I explore how algorithms can be interpreted as formal systems with precise logical behaviour. Concepts such as Gödel's incompleteness theorem highlight how mathematical systems themselves have limits, which deepens my interest in the foundations of computation.

Intellectual Interests

My academic interests lie at the intersection of mathematics and computer science. I am particularly interested in how abstract mathematical ideas translate into computational structures and algorithms.

Problems such as the Tower of Hanoi illustrate how recursion can generate complex behaviour from simple rules, while puzzles like the Lazy Susan problem highlight the role of symmetry and hidden information in algorithmic strategy. These explorations strengthened my interest in deeper questions about the nature of computation.

I am especially curious about the foundations of computer science, including mathematical logic, formal systems, and the relationship between proofs and programs. Ideas such as Gödel’s incompleteness theorem demonstrate that mathematics itself is not a closed system, but an evolving framework that continues to raise new questions.

Future Direction

I intend to pursue Computer Science with emphasis on theoretical foundations, abstraction, and mathematically informed approaches to computing systems and algorithms.

I am particularly interested in how mathematical reasoning informs program design, logic, and algorithmic thinking.
